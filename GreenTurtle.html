General Facts

Green turtles are a large sea turtle that are usually between 110kg - 190kg when fully grown however the largest found was 395kg. They have a smooth wide carapace or shell and usually grow up to 3-4 feet long and is heart shaped, however the largest found was 5 feet in length, which makes them the largest hard-shelled sea turtle.They are reptiles with the scientific name Chelonia mydas; Chelonia being the genus and mydas being the species. Despite its name a green sea turtle's shell is not always green, they can be a blend of different colours, including brown, olive, grey or black. The underside is a yellowish white colour with the turtles head having brown and yellow markings. Green turtles get their name from their body fat which is green. Unfortunately they are an endangered spieces and the current population trend is on the decline.

Habitat

Green turtles are found throughout the world however they are mainly found in tropical and subtropical waters where they stay near the coastline and around islands. Some locations are: Mesoamerican Reef, Coastal East Africa, Gulf of California, The Gal√°pagos, Coral Triangle living in bays and protected shores, especially in areas with seagrass beds. They have been observed clambering onto land to sunbathe and they are rarely found in the open ocean.

Reproduction

Green Turtles live for a long time, up to 70 years or more. Males mate with females on foraging grounds, along migratory pathways, and off nesting beaches. Female turtles reach maturity at 25 - 35 years old. Once matured, every 2 - 5 years they will migrate back to the beaches where they were hatched decades earlier. They dig a pit into the sand with their flippers, dig a pit where they lay 100 - 200 eggs, covers the pit and then returns to sea, they will leave the eggs to hatch after about 2 months. Once hatched they will make their way to the water. Hatchlings orient themselves seaward by moving away from the darkest silhouette of the landward dune or vegetation to crawl towards the brightest horizon. On underdeveloped beaches, this is toward the open horizon over the ocean.

Threats

Bycatch in fishing gear is a primary threat to green turtles, although unintended it can result in the turtles drowning or causing injuries that can lead to death or debilitation, examples are; swallowing hooks, or flippers getting entangeled in the nets.
Unfortunately green turtles were killed in large numbers for their fat, meat and eggs, which has lead to a catastrophic global decline of the species. While illegal in some countries, in others killing green turtles and collecting their eggs is still legal and this disrupts regional efforts to help the species recover from their endangered status.
The loss and degredation of nesting habitats due to coastal development and rising seas from climate change. Seawalls also known as shorline hardening or armouring can result in the loss of dry sand suitable for successful nesting. Artificial lighting on and near nesting beaches can deter females from coming ashore to nest and can disorient hatchlings trying to find the sea after emerging from their nests.

Threats Continued

Multiple different types of vessels can strike green turtles when they are near or at the surface resulting in injury or death. Unfortunately this is another major threat these turtles face, in particular large juveniles and adults near ports, waterways , and developed coastlines are the most at risk. High boat traffic areas such as marinas and inlets present a higher risk to green turtles. Nesting females are more susceptible to vessel strikes when making migrations to nest and whilst they are near shore during nesting season.
Increasing pollution of nearshore and offshore marine habitats threatens all sea turtles and degrades their habitats. Oil spills are some of the most damaging to their habitats and the turtles themselves. one of the most damaging being the Deepwater Horizon Oil Spill. Ingestion of marine debris such as fishing lines, balloons, plastic bags, floating tar, or oil and other discarded materials by humans can be mistaken for food. They can also become entangled in lost or discarded fishing gear, which can seriously injure or kill them.
A warming climate is likely to result in changes in beach morphology and higher sand temperatures which can be lethal to eggs or altar the ratio of male to female hatchlings. Beach erosion is caused by rising seas and storms, which may flood nests or wash them away. Changes in the temerature of the marine environment is likely to alter the abundance and distribution of food, leading to a shift in the migratory and foraging and nesting seasons of green turtles.
Fibropapillomatosis is a disease that causes external and internal tumors. These tumors can significantly affect their ablility to swim and feed and can lead to death. The disease is most common in green turtles and some evidence has linked the disease prevalence to degraded marine habitats.
